Game won't detect your joystick or wheel? The input bridge turns it into an Xbox controller

A racing game from the Microsoft Store, a Game Pass title or a flight game that stubbornly only accepts the Xbox controller – while your joystick, flight stick or older wheel stays invisible? The Lumora input bridge creates a virtual Xbox 360 controller from your device that every XInput game recognises instantly.

Why your device is "not detected"

Many modern Windows games – especially from the Microsoft Store and Game Pass – only speak XInput, the Xbox controller protocol. Joysticks, flight sticks, older wheels and pedals, however, report themselves as classic DirectInput devices. The game simply ignores them – not because the device is broken, but because the game never asks for them.

The input bridge translates in between: it reads your device and feeds the inputs into a virtual Xbox controller that behaves, to Windows and every game, like a real Xbox gamepad.

Who this is for

  • Racing games from the Microsoft Store / Game Pass – many don't detect DirectInput wheels, and Steam Input doesn't apply to Store titles. With the bridge you still steer with your wheel. (Verified with Assetto Corsa and BeamNG.drive, among others.)
  • Older or budget wheels on modern console ports – Logitech Driving Force, Thrustmaster & co. report as DirectInput; many ports only accept XInput. The bridge closes exactly this gap.
  • Flight games with a joystick / HOTAS – arcade flight titles that insist on a gamepad can be flown with your flight stick or throttle; analog axes land cleanly on the stick/trigger axes.
  • Sim-rig combos – merge wheel, separate pedals, handbrake and shifter into one virtual pad, even when the parts come from different manufacturers.
  • Accessibility – if you can't press an analog trigger, map full throttle to a single button; conversely an axis can trigger a button. Inputs bend freely to what your hand allows.
  • Fight sticks, arcade sticks, exotic USB controllers – anything Windows sees as an input device becomes an Xbox gamepad that every game understands.

Setup: click, move, done

  1. Pick your device – Lumora lists all connected joysticks, wheels and gamepads.
  2. Map it visually – click the button or axis on the pictured Xbox controller, then move the real lever or press the real button. Auto-calibration captures rest position and travel on its own – inverted axes and combined pedals included.
  3. Save a profile – linkable per game: when the game starts, the bridge switches on automatically (only if the device is connected too) and off again afterwards.
  • Axis → trigger: map a pedal or throttle onto the analog LT/RT – real analog input instead of on/off.
  • Button → axis: conversely a button can drive an axis to full deflection.
  • Per-game profiles with automatic activation on game start.
  • Everything local: no cloud, no account, no telemetry.

The difference from x360ce

The well-known x360ce works with DLL files copied into the game folder – which is exactly what's not possible with Microsoft Store and Game Pass games, whose folders are sealed. The input bridge instead relies on the signed open-source driver ViGEmBus: the virtual controller exists system-wide, for every game, without touching a single file in the game folder.

Honest limitation: vibration and force feedback are currently not passed back to your device. For a force-feedback wheel that your game supports natively, the manufacturer's driver remains the better choice – the bridge is for the cases where the game otherwise can't see your device at all.

What gets installed – and what doesn't

For the virtual controller, Windows needs the ViGEmBus driver once (signed, open source, by Nefarius). Lumora downloads it from the official source only after your click, verifies the signature and installs it – removable any time via "Apps & Features". Nothing happens automatically in the background, and as with everything in Lumora, the full source code is public on GitHub.
